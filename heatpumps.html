All Hail the Heat Pump

I’m an architect and engineer focused on the integration of clean technologies into the built environment. People in this space are increasingly into heat pumps. The popular understanding is that a heat pump can heat and cool a space more efficiently than traditional methods, especially when paired with other systems. But there is actually much more to it than that. A lot of chemistry and physics conspiring to make a beautiful, energy efficient, under-hyped creature of dreams.


Something from Nothing

Heat naturally spreads from areas of high to low concentration, meaning that it requires energy to make things hotter and to keep a hot thing hot. Heat pumps don’t violate this principle. But they are able to use a minimal amount of energy to transfer a large amount of heat. Mischievous little devils that they are, heat pumps steal existing heat from one place and swindle it into another. (The air has a lot of heat in it, even cold air, as long as it’s warmer than absolute zero degrees Kelvin, or -273ºC.)

The same principles that lie behind heat pump technologies for buildings also lie behind refrigerators and air conditioning units. Heat pumps can be used to heat water, rather than air, for showers and dishwashers and everything else. Companies are starting to use these ideas to produce concentrated heat for industrial steam. The applications abound.

But outside of the building technology space, heat pumps don’t often get recognized as a sexy climate solution; one that can be a zero-emissions process if powered with renewable energy, and one whose efficiency still can be greatly improved with the development of better systems. This is at least partially due to the fact that most people assume a heat pump is simply using electricity to produce heat, rather than using a small amount of electricity and a large amount of thermodynamics to transfer heat from one place to another.

This is a crash course in thermodynamic principles specific to heat pumps. These principles are fairly complicated, but even just knowing of the complicatedness is enough to inspire awe.


Zoomed Out

Heat pumps do work to manipulate the pressure of a refrigerant, which ultimately results in heat transfer due to the refrigerant’s phase change.

We begin the cycle within the expansion device . The pressure on the refrigerant is decreased, so it becomes a part-vapor, part-liquid with a temperature below the ambient temperature outside.

The refrigerant then travels through the evaporator . Here, heat energy from the air outside transfers to the refrigerant, adding energy to transform the refrigerant into a gaseous state.

The compressor then does work to add pressure to the gaseous refrigerant. This causes an increase in the refrigerant’s temperature.

Afterwards, the refrigerant flows inside through the condenser . The refrigerant liquifies, releasing energy as heat into the inside air. It then enters the expansion device to begin the process again.

Note: in an air conditioner or refrigerator, this cycle would be reversed, moving heat from the inside out.


Zoomed In

The central player in this all is the refrigerant. There are a slew of refrigerants, most with names consisting of the letter R-somenumbers and dyed a scary bright color. They have other properties in common as well, the most important of which is a low boiling point. This is optimal because it means refrigerants vaporize more easily than other substances. This vaporization is what traps energy to release as heat later on, at a higher pressure.

Some refrigerants are toxic. They also can be harmful in other, more insidious ways. Remember the ozone hole? In the 1970s, refrigerators and air conditioning units in landfills began degrading, releasing their CFC refrigerants into the atmosphere, where Cl and Br ions helped catalyze the natural breakdown of ozone (O3) molecules into O2 molecules, destroying the Earth’s natural UV umbrella. CFCs and HCFCs got phased out in favor of HFCs, which don’t degrade the ozone layer but do have hundreds of times the heat-trapping power of carbon dioxide. The Kigali Amendment commits to the phase-out of HFCs in favor of natural refrigerants such as ammonia, propane, isobutane, and carbon dioxide itself. All this is to say that many refrigerants that are effective at cooling are not suitable for use in the real world.

Of those that are, each has a unique set of behaviors under different temperatures and pressures. These swirly charts of eddies and vortexes may seem to be jumbles of information difficult to digest. But they really are a lovely way to keep track of what changes in pressure and enthalpy mean for a substance’s temperature and entropy.

First, some definitions:

Pressure (Pascals): measured on the y-axis. Describes how much stress is on the system. Can be increased by doing work and can be decreased when the system does work on something else.

Enthalpy (Joules per kilogram): measured on the x-axis. A measure of the total energy in a system. Equal to //hover to show equation with components labeled// the system’s internal energy plus the product of its volume and pressure.

Temperature (degrees Kelvin): perhaps the most intuitive of these concepts. Measured with your regular old thermometer. Temperature lines run horizontally through the liquid-vapor mix region of the diagram and curve away at the edges. Usually increases with enthalpy, but not when a substance is changing state; through the vapor mixing area, the temperature lines are horizontal because increases in enthalpy go towards vaporizing the substance, rather than increasing its temperature.

Entropy (Joules per Kelvin): represented by the verticalish lines on the chart,. In this scenario can be described as how vaporized, or disordered, the liquid-vapor mixture is. The thermodynamic identity //hover// shows that the internal energy of a substance is related to a temperature entropy term and a pressure volume term.
